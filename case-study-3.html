Rebuilding a growth engine without buying ads.

A fourteen-week investigation into why a category-leading product had stopped compounding — and the embarrassingly simple loop that finally fixed it.

Chapter IOpening
Scene

A board deck arrives. The growth chart has flattened politely.

Setting Halo & Co — a Series B productivity tool with 180,000 active users and a quarter of polite growth. Stakes A planned C round priced on the previous compounding rate. Either the curve bent up again, or the round priced down.

When a SaaS company calls a brand person instead of a growth person, it usually means they suspect the problem is not arithmetic. Halo's CEO had spent six months hiring against the chart and four months watching the chart not move. She didn't need another paid acquisition lead. She needed someone willing to question the entire story the company was telling itself.

"We don't have a growth problem. We have a meaning problem. And it's showing up on the dashboard."
Chapter IIThe
Conflict

The funnel was full. The product was loved. The loop was broken.

Symptoms Strong top-of-funnel. Decent activation. Inviting stalled at 6%. Net revenue retention was healthy enough to disguise the leak. Hidden cost Every dollar of paid acquisition was, in effect, replacing the referrals that should have been happening for free.

The first week was archeology. We read every onboarding email of the previous two years, watched 38 hours of session replays, and interviewed every PM about why a particular feature shipped. A pattern emerged: the product had been quietly rebuilt for power users over four releases, and the moment new users typically said "oh, this is for me" had moved seventeen screens deeper.

The growth engine wasn't broken. It was being run further and further from the part of the product that earned word-of-mouth.

Chapter IIIThe
Investigation

We ran three honest hypotheses. Two failed.

Discipline Every hypothesis got a pre-registered success metric and a kill date. No moving goalposts. No "directional" wins.

The team had been quietly afraid to run experiments that might fail. I told them failure was not optional — it was the entire point. We picked three hypotheses, gave each a deadline, and agreed in advance what we would do if the number didn't move.

Hypothesis A · Failed

Better paid creative will reignite acquisition.

Tested 24 new ad variants across two channels. CPMs dropped, CACs held flat. The audience was tapped, not under-served.

— retire paid hypothesis
Hypothesis B · Failed

A pricing change will unlock pent-up demand.

Tested two new tiers and a free workspace. Net revenue moved 4%. Activation didn't move at all.

— pricing isn't the lever
Hypothesis C · Won

Move the "aha" moment two screens forward.

Rebuilt the first 90 seconds of the product around one specific outcome — the one users described in interviews as "the thing I told my team about."

+186% activation in 11 days.
Chapter IVThe Turning
Point

The loop that finally felt like the product.

The loop Aha → Invite (in-product, contextual) → Co-activation → Aha (again, together). What we removed Three paid channels, one referral discount, one swag drop, one "growth team" Slack channel.

Once new users hit the redesigned "aha" moment, the right ask became obvious: "who on your team would this be useful with?" Not a referral. A collaboration. The invite stopped being a growth tactic and started being a product action.

Within four weeks, the loop's K-factor settled at 2.7×. Inviting climbed from 6% to 31%. We turned off three paid channels to see what would happen. The growth chart kept bending.

Chapter VThe
Outcome

The C round priced up. The team got smaller.

Six months later Activated users +186%. Paid spend down 41%. Round priced 1.3× the previous valuation. Growth team reorganized as a product squad.

The investigation was supposed to last fourteen weeks. It lasted thirteen. The deliverable was supposed to be a deck. It turned out to be a redesigned product onboarding and an honest conversation about which channels the company should stop pretending mattered.

Six months later, the chart was compounding again. — quietly, this time.
