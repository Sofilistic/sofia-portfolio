Brand · Heritage Rebrand · 2023

Newer, by being more itself.

A 40-year-old neighborhood bakery on the wrong side of a generational handover. We mined eight decades of receipts, photographs, and family arguments to build a brand that felt newer by feeling more itself.

Chapter IOpening
Scene

The founder's son inherits the bakery and a quiet panic.

Setting A four-decade-old bakery on a corner that had quietly become trendy around it. Tension The new owner — the founder's son — could either chase the neighborhood, or remind it where it came from.

Mr. Olufemi opened the bakery in 1984 with two ovens, one recipe, and a hand-painted sign. By 2023, he had retired, the sign had been replaced twice, and his son had inherited a business that everyone said was "iconic" — which, in his experience, meant nobody under thirty actually came in.

He had been quoted three rebrands, each one cleaner than the last. Each one would have erased exactly what made the bakery worth saving.

Chapter IIThe
Conflict

"Modernizing" meant deleting the bakery.

What the other pitches wanted Sans-serif wordmark. Sage-and-cream palette. A pivot to "artisanal toast culture." What the bakery actually was A loud, warm, hand-painted, occasionally-running-late, three-generation institution.

Every previous proposal had treated the bakery's history as a liability to be softened. I argued, in the first meeting, that the history was the only thing the competition couldn't copy. The competition was, in fact, doing an excellent job copying everything else about the bakery.

"A heritage brand doesn't need to be made modern. It needs to be made legible."
Chapter IIIThe
Investigation

We spent three weeks in the family's storage closet.

Archive Forty years of receipts, two photo albums, a binder of menus, a videotape from the 20-year party, three hand-painted signs in the basement, one argument about a pie. Output A 90-page brand archive that doubled as the strategy.

Brand strategy, for heritage businesses, is mostly an act of listening backwards. The family had kept everything. Nobody had ever asked to see it.

  • We found a 1989 menu, hand-lettered, that contained the exact tone of voice we needed.
  • We found a photograph of Mr. Olufemi in a red apron — the red that became the brand's only color.
  • We found a receipt with a customer's handwritten note on the back: "this is the bread my mother makes." That sentence became the wordmark's tagline.

Forty years, condensed

A bakery told as a timeline.

1984

The first oven, one recipe.

Mr. Olufemi opens on a quiet Tuesday. The hand-painted sign goes up the next morning.

1991

The neighborhood arrives.

A second oven. A second baker. A reputation that travels by foot.

2004

The first rebrand. (We forgive it.)

A well-intentioned redesign sands down the warmth. Foot traffic dips. The original sign moves to the basement.

2019

The neighborhood becomes the audience.

Three new bakeries open within four blocks. Each looks, sounds, and tastes the same as the others.

2023

We open the storage closet.

The rebrand becomes an act of restoration. The red apron returns. The hand-painted sign returns. The mother returns.

Chapter IVThe Turning
Point

Re-installing the 1984 sign — this time on purpose.

The brand system One red. One hand-lettered wordmark, redrawn from the 1989 menu. One photography style — natural light, no styling, taken on the founder's son's old camera. The opening A Saturday breakfast for the neighborhood. Free coffee. The original sign, restored, unveiled at 8am.

The launch was small on purpose. We invited the eighty-six regulars whose names were in the order book, plus the local paper, plus three neighborhood food writers who had never been asked anywhere by anyone. We did not invite any influencers.

The Sunday paper ran a full-page feature the following weekend. The line started forming at 7:45am. ↑ this is the only KPI I cared about that day.

The competition was modern. We were inherited.

Chapter VThe
Outcome

Twelve months later, three generations were back in the shop.

Year one Foot traffic +74%. Unprompted press in seven publications. A two-page feature in the Sunday supplement. Two of the three competitor bakeries had quietly closed by the end of 2024.

The bakery did not become trendy. The bakery became recognized — which is a much more useful thing for a forty-year-old business to be. The owners did not need to win a new audience. They needed the audience that was already walking past to notice them again.

The hardest thing about heritage work is convincing the client to trust what they already have. The most rewarding thing about heritage work is watching them, eight months later, treat that history like the asset it always was.
