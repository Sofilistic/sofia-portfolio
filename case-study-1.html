Chapter I

The brief: Launch a commodity product.
The challenge: making people care.


Setting A pharmacy aisle. Twenty versions of the same product. No reason to choose one over another. Tension Consumers had learned to tolerate poor quality cotton wool. Cotton shed lint, absorbed too much product, and came in packaging that exposed it to dust after a single use. Nobody expected better, which made charging a premium feel impossible.

Most cotton wool brands competed on the same things: price, availability, and habit. Walk into any pharmacy and you'd find twenty versions of the same product, packaged differently but promising little. Consumers had learned to tolerate lint, contamination, and waste because the category had convinced them those problems were normal.

Cleanelle was built on a different premise. High-density, lint-free cotton balls. Resealable packaging. A cleaner, more reliable experience from first use to last. The product solved a real problem, but products rarely win because they're better. The challenge was making people notice what they'd spent years ignoring, and giving them a reason to a better product.

Chapter II

The category had trained consumers not to care.

What was failing Commodity thinking. Every brand competed on availability and affordability, leaving quality as an afterthought.

The category had spent years convincing consumers that cotton wool was too ordinary to improve. Packaging was flimsy. Product quality was inconsistent and because the price point was low, brands assumed nobody cared enough to notice.

But people did notice. Beauty professionals noticed when cotton fibres ruined a finish, skincare users noticed when products disappeared into over-absorbent cotton. The problem was that nobody had built a brand around solving those frustrations. What looked like a commodity was actually a trust problem hiding in plain sight.

That meant the launch had to feel less like a commodity launch and more like a better standard to an overlooked problem.

Chapter III

I replaced "good enough" with a reason to switch.

Methods Category audits. Consumer interviews. Retail observation. One uncomfortable amount of time staring at pharmacy shelves. Output A six-month launch strategy built on a single idea: people deserved better than "good enough."

The more research I did, the less interested I became in cotton wool itself and the more interested I became in the habits surrounding it. The category had spent decades teaching consumers that cotton wool was interchangeable. Pick any pack, get roughly the same experience, move on. The audience was redefined not by demographics but by moments:

  • Removing makeup after a long day.
  • Cleaning and dressing a wound
  • Finishing a manicure
Chapter IVThe
Outcome

The entire strategy rested on a simple bet: if consumers could see the difference, they would pay for it.

PROJECTED 6-MONTH MODEL 100 salon partners. 25 retail locations. ₦13.6M revenue potential. A built-in refill loop designed for repeat purchase behavior.

Every category develops assumptions over time. In this one, the assumption was that consumers didn't care enough to notice the difference. The strategy challenged that premise.

Instead of treating cotton as a commodity, we treated it as a product people touch directly on their skin, use in their routines, and trust in moments where hygiene matters. Once viewed through that lens, the path forward became obvious. Don't compete on price instead, give people a reason to expect better. ↑ this is the whole point.
