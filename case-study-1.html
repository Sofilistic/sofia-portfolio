A candle, a confession.

How we launched a $48 candle by writing fewer ads — and let the audience finish the story.

Chapter IOpening
Scene

A founder calls on a Sunday. She is quietly furious.

Setting Brooklyn, late August. A 14-person team, a beautiful product, and a launch calendar that has slipped twice. Tension Two competitors raised — loudly. Three agencies pitched the same "lifestyle-led performance" deck. Nobody asked what the candle was for.

The founder of Maison Rouge had spent four years and most of her savings developing a candle that smelled like the apartment her grandmother kept in Marseille — fig leaves, old paper, the particular dust of an afternoon spent waiting. She did not need another agency to tell her it was "premium." She needed someone to tell her why a stranger would spend forty-eight dollars on a memory that wasn't theirs.

The brief, when it arrived, was twelve pages of mood. There was no positioning, no audience, no math. There was, instead, a short story about her grandmother. I read it three times on the train.

"I don't want a launch. I want one beautiful argument, made well, that I can stand behind for ten years."
Chapter IIThe
Conflict

The category had become loud, identical, exhausting.

What was failing Performance creative as the only growth lever. 60+ static ad variations per launch. Average shelf life: 9 days. What the data missed Attention quality. People were watching candles the way you watch a stranger's wallpaper.

I spent two weeks reading every candle launch of the prior eighteen months. They had merged into a single ad: a hand pouring wax in a beige kitchen, three SKUs fanned out on a marble counter, a voiceover that began with the word introducing. None of them said anything about who the candle was for, because none of them believed the candle was for anyone in particular.

Our investigation surfaced an uncomfortable truth: the audience for $48 candles was not "affluent women 28–44 interested in wellness." It was a much smaller, much weirder group — people for whom buying a candle was a way of buying a feeling they could not name. A consoling purchase. Almost a ritual.

Mood board, week 2
Audience interview map

That meant the launch had to feel less like a product reveal and more like an invitation into a small, private universe.

Chapter IIIThe
Investigation

We replaced the brief with a short film script.

Methods 14 long-form audience interviews. 1 photo-diary study. Competitive teardown across 38 brands. One night re-reading Annie Ernaux for tone. Output A nine-page strategy doc shaped like a screenplay treatment.

I have a quiet rule: if the strategy can't be read aloud at dinner without making someone lean in, it isn't a strategy yet. So I rewrote the brief as a treatment — a single scene, a single character, a single argument about what the candle did to a room.

  • The audience was redefined as "people who light a candle the way other people pour a drink."
  • The hero unit was repositioned from a candle to a 90-second film with three quiet edits — one for paid, one for organic, one for the founder's mother.
  • The launch funnel was cut from 60+ variations to seven assets, total. Each one had to earn its place.

The team thought I was bluffing. The founder thought I was reading her mind. We shot the film in three days on 16mm, in an apartment that did not belong to anyone in particular.

— Chapter break —

We stopped trying to interrupt people. We started trying to arrive at the right moment.

M.O.
Chapter IVThe Turning
Point

Day three of the launch: the wrong people stopped scrolling.

Channel mix Meta (paid social), one editorial newsletter placement, two podcasts, a handwritten note to 41 press contacts. No Influencer codes. Discounts. "Founder story" carousels.

On day three, the unsubscribe rate from our email list dropped to a number I had not seen in seven years of marketing. People were forwarding the film to friends with two words: "this you?"

The campaign was not, in any traditional sense, "going viral." It was doing something subtler and far more useful — it was making a specific kind of person feel seen, and that person was telling exactly one other person.

The first 4,000 customers were not bought. They were recognized.

By the end of week two, the warehouse was rationing inventory. By the end of week four, two retailers we had not pitched were emailing the founder directly.

Chapter VThe
Outcome

The numbers were the least interesting part.

12-month follow-up Repeat purchase rate held at 38%. No paid creative refresh for six months. Two retailers, one feature in a major Sunday supplement.

Launch month came in at +312% of forecast on a creative budget 61% smaller than the founder's previous launch. Blended ROAS held at 4.2× through week four; by week eight, organic was outperforming paid two to one.

But the number I keep returning to is this: in twelve months, the brand spent zero dollars refreshing the launch film. The argument we made on day one was still doing the work. ↑ this is the whole point.
